
### **Part 1: 25 Short-Answer Questions (Definitions & Concepts)**

**1. What is the primary purpose of evaluation in the HCI design process?** The purpose of evaluation is to evaluate designs and test systems to ensure they work as intended and satisfy user expectations. It is not a stand-alone step but should occur at every stage of the design process, with findings influencing necessary design changes. The three primary goals are to determine the scope of functionality, gauge user interaction, and pinpoint specific design problems.

**2. Define the "Cognitive Walkthrough" method.** Originally proposed by Polson, the cognitive walkthrough is an expert evaluation method where evaluators "step through" a sequence of actions for a specific task to identify potential usability problems. Its primary objective is to determine how simple a system is to learn via exploration. Evaluators generate a "story" explaining why each step is appropriate or inappropriate for new users.

**3. What is "Heuristic Evaluation" as developed by Nielsen and Molich?** Heuristic evaluation is a flexible, inexpensive usability engineering method where several experts independently analyze a system against a set of broadly applicable "heuristics" or rules of thumb. It can be applied to design specifications, storyboards, prototypes, or operational systems. The goal is to identify deviations from these heuristics that might point to potential usability issues.

**4. Define "Laboratory Studies" in the context of evaluation.** Laboratory studies involve removing users from their regular work environments to participate in controlled tests within a specialized usability laboratory. This environment allows for advanced audio/visual recording, two-way mirrors, and distraction-free interaction. While highly accurate in recording events, they may lack the context and natural interruptions found in the real world.

**5. What are "Field Studies" and why are they used?** Field studies involve sending the evaluator into the user’s actual workspace to observe the system in action. This style preserves the natural context of the work, including high ambient noise and frequent interruptions like phone calls. It is the preferred method for seeing interactions in real-world settings and identifying how users store and restore state while performing tasks.

**6. Define "Independent Variables" in experimental evaluation.** Independent variables are the specific components of an experiment that the evaluator changes or manipulates to create different comparison conditions. Examples include the style of the interface, the number of items in a menu, or the design of an icon. Each specific value applied to an independent variable during an experiment is referred to as its "level".

**7. Define "Dependent Variables" in experimental evaluation.** Dependent variables are the elements that are measured during an experiment, as their values are expected to be "dependent" on the changes made to the independent variables. Common choices for measurement include the time taken to complete a task, the number of errors committed by the user, and subjective measures like user preference or satisfaction.

**8. What is a "Null Hypothesis" in HCI experiments?** A hypothesis is a prediction of an experiment's outcome, while the null hypothesis specifically claims that there is no measurable difference in the dependent variable across the different levels of the independent variable. The goal of the experiment is to use statistical measures to disprove the null hypothesis, showing that observed differences did not happen by chance.

**9. Define "Between-Subjects" experimental design.** In a between-subjects (or randomized) design, each participant is assigned to only one experimental condition. This approach controls for "learning effects" where a user might perform better on a second task simply because they practiced on the first. However, it requires larger sample sizes and is susceptible to skewing if there is large individual variance between participants.

**10. Define "Within-Subjects" experimental design.** Within-subjects (or repeated measures) design involves every user performing under every experimental condition. This method requires fewer participants and reduces the impact of individual user variation. To mitigate transfer-of-learning effects, evaluators often use "counterbalancing," where different groups of users complete the tasks in a different order.

**11. What is "Think Aloud" observation?** Think aloud is an observational technique where the user is asked to describe what they are doing and why while interacting with a system. The user may explain their goals, their interpretations of what is happening on the screen, and the reasons for their specific actions. It provides helpful insight into interface issues and user decision-making processes.

**12. What is "Cooperative Evaluation"?** Cooperative evaluation is a variation of the think-aloud process where the user is viewed as a full collaborator in the evaluation rather than just an experimental subject. The evaluator is allowed to ask the user "why" or "what-if" questions to clarify confusing behaviors, and the user can ask the evaluator for clarification when problems arise during the session.

**13. Define "Protocol Analysis" in user observation.** Protocol analysis is the systematic recording and documentation of an evaluation session to create a "protocol" for later study. Recording methods include paper and pencil notes, audio recording for "think aloud" comments, video recording to capture physical actions, and computer logging to track every keystroke or mouse movement.

**14. What is the "Post-task Walkthrough"?** A post-task walkthrough occurs after an observation session, where the participant and analyst review the recorded transcript or video together. The participant is given the opportunity to explain the rationale behind specific behaviors or clarify what went wrong when errors occurred. This is vital for critical tasks where "thinking aloud" would be too disruptive.

**15. Define "Query Techniques" in evaluation.** Query techniques involve asking the user directly about their perspective, needs, and preferences regarding an interface. Unlike observation, these methods rely on the user's subjective report to highlight issues the designer may have missed. The two primary categories used in HCI research are interviews and questionnaires.

**16. What is the difference between open-ended and closed questions in questionnaires?** Open-ended questions allow users to provide unprompted, subjective feedback in their own words, which is useful for gathering broad information and suggestions. Closed questions—such as scalar, multi-choice, or ranked questions—provide specific options for the user to choose from. Closed questions are easier to analyze and compare rigorously.

**17. Define "Typography" in visual design.** Typography is the art and technique of arranging typefaces to make written language legible, readable, and visually appealing. It involves selecting fonts and determining elements like size, color, leading (line spacing), kerning (letter spacing), and tracking. Effective typography establishes visual hierarchy and guides the user through the content.

**18. Define "Iconography" in HCI.** Iconography is the use of symbols or images to communicate ideas, represent concepts, or initiate actions within a user interface. Icons serve as a universal language that can overcome linguistic barriers and provide visual efficiency in interfaces with limited space. They act as wayfinding indicators that help users navigate complex information structures.

**19. What is "Responsive Web Design"?** Responsive web design is an approach where design and development adapt to the user’s behavior and environment based on screen size, platform, and orientation. It utilizes flexible grids, layouts, and CSS media queries to ensure a website transitions seamlessly between devices like laptops, tablets, and smartphones. This eliminates the need for separate designs for every new device.

**20. What is "Visual Acuity" in screen design?** Visual acuity refers to the capacity of the human eye for perceiving fine detail. In evaluation and design, this is measured using the "visual angle," which accounts for an object's size and its distance from the eye. Understanding visual acuity limits helps designers ensure that text and icons are legible to users.

**21. Define "Affordance" regarding screen layout.** Affordance is the psychological concept that an object’s physical or visual characteristics indicate what can be done with it. For example, a button’s shape should "afford" pushing. Designers leverage these affordances to create intuitive interfaces, though they must remember that these cues are often culturally or historically specific.

**22. What is "Universal Design" in HCI?** Universal design is the practice of designing products and interactive systems so they can be used by as many people as possible, in as many situations as possible. It aims to ensure that technology is accessible to users of all skill levels and physical abilities, without requiring specialized design or adaptation.

**23. Define "Formative Evaluation."** Formative evaluation is a type of assessment carried out throughout the design process with the specific intent of improving the design. It identifies usability issues early so they can be corrected before significant resources are committed to implementation.

**24. Define "Summative Evaluation."** Summative evaluation is conducted at the end of the development process to verify that the final product meets established quality standards and user requirements. It provides an overall assessment of the system's effectiveness, efficiency, and user satisfaction.

**25. What is "Task Analysis"?** Task analysis is the process of examining how people carry out their work, including what they do, what tools they use, and what knowledge they require. It focuses on user performance rather than computer performance and is often used to create detailed scenarios for testing prototypes.

### **Part 2: 10 Long-Answer Questions (Theories, Models, & Processes)**

**1. Describe the four essential items and four key questions required for a Cognitive Walkthrough.** To perform a cognitive walkthrough, evaluators must have four specific pieces of information ready before starting the review process:

- **A system prototype or detailed specification** to show the interface's appearance.
- **A description of a representative task** that the user is expected to complete.
- **A complete list of the actions** required to complete that task.
- **An identification of the target users** and their expected experience level.

During the walkthrough, evaluators ask these four questions at every step of the action sequence:

- **Is the effect of the action the same as the user’s goal?** (Does the system's outcome match what the user is trying to do?).
- **Will users see that the action is available?** (Is the button or menu item visible?).
- **Will users know it is the correct action?** (Once found, is the meaning of the button clear?).
- **Will users understand the feedback?** (Can the user confirm the goal was achieved?).

**2. Detail Nielsen’s Ten Heuristics for interface evaluation.** Heuristic evaluation uses these ten broadly applicable rules to identify usability flaws:

- **Visibility of system status:** Users should always be informed about what is happening through timely feedback.
- **Match between system and the real world:** Use words, phrases, and concepts familiar to the user rather than system-specific jargon.
- **User control and freedom:** Provide an "emergency exit" to undo accidental actions without a long dialogue.
- **Consistency and standards:** Follow platform conventions so the same words or actions always mean the same thing.
- **Error prevention:** Design the system to prevent problems from occurring in the first place.
- **Recognition rather than recall:** Minimize memory load by making objects and actions visible.
- **Flexibility and efficiency of use:** Include accelerators (like shortcuts) that allow expert users to work faster.
- **Aesthetic and minimalist design:** Do not include irrelevant information that competes with important data.
- **Help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors:** Error messages should be in plain language and suggest a solution.
- **Help and documentation:** If needed, documentation should be easy to search and focused on the user's tasks.

**3. Explain the "Formality Gap" and its impact on validation and verification.** Bridging the difference between the real world and mathematical logic is a central challenge in the software process:

- **Verification (The System Perspective):** Occurs within formal languages to ensure internal consistency ("building the thing right").
- **Validation (The User Perspective):** Demonstrates that the design meets the customer's actual real-world needs ("building the right thing").
- **The Formality Gap:** There is an inherent jump between unstructured real-world requirements and formal, structured development approaches.
- **Subjectivity:** Because real-world ambiguity cannot be fully captured in formal logic, validation must rely on subjective methods and expert interpretation.

**4. Compare the advantages and disadvantages of Laboratory Studies versus Field Studies.** Choosing between these two styles depends on the goals of the evaluation:

- **Laboratory Studies - Advantages:** Access to advanced recording equipment, distraction-free environment, and the ability to perform highly controlled comparisons.
- **Laboratory Studies - Disadvantages:** Lack of natural context, such as office interruptions or physical tools, which may result in behavior that never happens in the real world.
- **Field Studies - Advantages:** Preservation of natural context and social interactions, allowing observers to see how users really manage tasks and interruptions.
- **Field Studies - Disadvantages:** High ambient noise, difficulty in recording high-quality audio/video, and the evaluators' presence may distract the users.

**5. Outline the process of Designing a Controlled Experiment for evaluation.** A rigorous experiment must follow a specific structural logic:

- **Select a Hypothesis:** State a clear prediction about how a change in the interface will affect user behavior.
- **Identify Variables:** Define the **Independent Variables** (what you change) and the **Dependent Variables** (what you measure).
- **Choose a Design:** Decide between **Between-Subjects** (different users for each condition) or **Within-Subjects** (same users for all conditions).
- **Select Participants:** Ensure the group represents the intended user base in terms of age, education, and computer experience.
- **Perform Statistical Analysis:** Use parametric or non-parametric tests to determine if the results are significant or due to chance.

**6. Describe the levels of interaction that must be considered during Navigation Design.** When designing the structure of an application, users interact at four distinct levels:

- **Widgets:** Selecting the right buttons, menus, and text fields for individual actions.
- **Screens or Windows:** Logically grouping these widgets and ensuring the layout is easy to scan.
- **Navigation within the Application:** Ensuring users understand where they are in the interaction and what happens when they press a button.
- **Environment:** Considering how the application interacts with the operating system, other programs, and distant networks.

**7. Discuss the Seven Principles of Universal Design.** These principles provide a framework for creating systems accessible to all:

- **Equitable Use:** The design is useful and marketable to people with diverse abilities.
- **Flexibility in Use:** The design accommodates a wide range of individual preferences and abilities.
- **Simple and Intuitive Use:** Usage is easy to understand regardless of the user's experience or concentration level.
- **Perceptible Information:** The design communicates necessary information effectively to the user regardless of ambient conditions.
- **Tolerance for Error:** The system minimizes hazards and adverse consequences of accidental actions.
- **Low Physical Effort:** The system can be used efficiently and comfortably with minimum fatigue.
- **Size and Space for Approach and Use:** Appropriate size and space is provided for approach and manipulation regardless of user mobility.

**8. Explain the role of "Task Analysis" in the design and evaluation phases.** Task analysis is a foundational user-centered design activity:

- **Requirements Analysis:** It identifies the actual tasks users need to perform, which informs the initial design.
- **Task Decomposition:** It breaks large tasks (e.g., "clean house") into smaller, manageable subtasks and plans for their execution.
- **Prototype Evaluation:** Tasks are used to create detailed "walkthrough" scenarios to see if a user has the knowledge to complete each step in a prototype.
- **Knowledge Identification:** It uncovers the specific knowledge users need about tools and procedures to be successful.

**9. Detail the process of using the "Think Aloud" technique for user testing.** Think aloud is one of the most direct ways to understand user cognition:

- **The Session:** The user is asked to complete a task while verbalizing their thought process, goals, and interpretations.
- **Recording:** The session is recorded using "Protocol Analysis" methods like video, audio, and computer logging.
- **Transcription:** The analyst creates a transcript including spoken words and non-verbal cues like pauses or frustration.
- **Analysis:** The evaluator looks for instances where the user’s mental model conflicts with the system’s behavior.
- **Post-task review:** If the task was too demanding to talk during, a "Post-task Walkthrough" is used to reflect on the recording with the user.

**10. Explain the factors an evaluator must consider when "Choosing an Evaluation Method."** Selecting the right technique requires balancing eight distinct factors:

- **Stage of the Cycle:** Design-stage evaluations are usually quick and analytical, while implementation-stage evaluations involve users.
- **Style of Evaluation:** Choosing between controlled laboratory studies or naturalistic field studies.
- **Subjectivity/Objectivity:** Balancing interpretation-based methods (like walkthroughs) with data-based methods (like experiments).
- **Type of Measures:** Deciding between quantitative (numerical) or qualitative (descriptive) data.
- **Information Level:** Determining if low-level detail (fonts) or high-level information (usability) is needed.
- **Immediacy and Intrusiveness:** Assessing how much the evaluation process itself will interfere with the user's natural behavior.
- **Required Resources:** Considering the time, equipment, and expertise available to the team.

### **Part 3: 6 Applied/Design Tasks (Scenarios & Analysis)**

**1. Designing an Experimental Evaluation**

- **Scenario:** A company wants to know if a "Circular Menu" is faster for users than a standard "List Menu."
- **Steps:**
    1. **Formulate Hypothesis:** "Users will select items faster from a circular menu than a list menu".
    2. **Define Variables:** Independent variable is the "Menu Style" (List vs. Circular). Dependent variable is "Selection Time" in milliseconds.
    3. **Experimental Design:** Use a **Within-Subjects** design so the same users test both menus, reducing individual variance.
    4. **Counterbalance:** Have half the users start with the List Menu and half start with the Circular Menu to prevent learning bias.
    5. **Statistical Analysis:** Apply a **Student’s t-test** on the difference of means to see if the speed difference is significant.

**2. Performing a Heuristic Evaluation**

- **Scenario:** You are evaluating a new e-commerce checkout page.
- **Steps:**
    1. **Select Evaluators:** Hire 3-5 usability experts to review the page independently.
    2. **Apply Heuristics:** Check if the page has a clear "System Status" (e.g., a progress bar showing steps 1, 2, 3).
    3. **Check Consistency:** Ensure the "Buy" button is the same color and in the same position on every screen.
    4. **Error Prevention:** Ensure the system checks for valid zip codes _before_ the user clicks submit.
    5. **Assign Severity:** Rate each identified issue from 0 (not a problem) to 4 (usability catastrophe) to prioritize fixes.

**3. Conducting a Cognitive Walkthrough**

- **Scenario:** Test the "Send a Message" feature in a new smartphone app.
- **Steps:**
    1. **Define the Action Sequence:** (1) Tap name, (2) Type message, (3) Press 'Send' icon.
    2. **Analyze Step 1:** Ask: "Will the user see the name list?" and "Will they know to tap it?".
    3. **Analyze Step 3:** Ask: "Is the 'Send' icon recognizable?" and "Does the user get feedback that the message was sent?".
    4. **Document Failures:** If the user doesn't know the icon is a "Send" button, record it as a usability problem on a report sheet.

**4. Designing for Different Screens (Responsive Analysis)**

- **Scenario:** Convert a desktop news site for use on a smartphone.
- **Steps:**
    1. **Adopt Responsive Grid:** Use fluid layouts that automatically resize columns based on screen width.
    2. **Mobile-First Priority:** Design for the smallest screen first, focusing on essential news headlines.
    3. **Adaptable Navigation:** Change the top menu bar into a "Hamburger" collapsible menu for smaller screens.
    4. **Touch Interaction:** Ensure buttons are large enough for thumb-tapping and spaced to prevent accidental touches.
    5. **Test Breakpoints:** Verify the layout shifts correctly at specific screen widths (e.g., 320px, 768px, 1024px).

**5. Hierarchical Task Analysis (HTA) for "Booking a Flight"**

- **Task:** Analyze the process of booking a flight online.
- **Textual Visual Flow:**
    - **Root Task 0:** Book a flight.
    - **Plan 0:** Do 1; then 2; then 3; when successful do 4.
    - **Subtask 1:** Enter search criteria (Dates, Destination).
    - **Subtask 2:** Select flight from list.
    - **Subtask 3:** Enter passenger and payment details.
    - **Subtask 4:** Confirm booking and save receipt.

**6. Applying Layout Tools (Grouping and Alignment)**

- **Scenario:** Design a "Medical Emergency Intake" screen.
- **Steps:**
    1. **Logical Grouping:** Place "Patient Identity" at the top and "Symptom Checklist" in a separate section.
    2. **Use White Space:** Leave a clear "counter" (gap) between the patient’s personal info and the medical info to draw attention to each.
    3. **Left-Align Labels:** Ensure all text labels (Name, Age, BP) are left-aligned for rapid scanning.
    4. **Right-Align Numbers:** Ensure blood pressure and temperature values are right-aligned to allow nurses to quickly compare magnitudes.
    5. **Provide Affordances:** Use raised-edge button styles for "Submit" so it is clearly actionable under stress.
