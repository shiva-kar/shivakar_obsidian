
### **Part 1: 25 Short-Answer Questions (Definitions & Concepts)**

**1. What is the basic definition of Interaction Design (IxD)?** Interaction design is a specialised field within Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) that concentrates on shaping user experiences with digital systems. It involves more than just creating a computer program or physical gadget; it determines how that object will impact people’s daily tasks. The end result is intended to be an intervention that alters the current situation for the better.

**2. Define the "Golden Rule of Design" in HCI.** The design golden rule is to "Understand your Materials". In human-computer interaction, the two most obvious materials are the human and the computer. This requires the designer to understand computers—including their limitations, capacities, and platforms—and people, including their psychological and social aspects and the inevitability of human error.

**3. What are "Stakeholders" in the design process?** Stakeholders are the numerous individuals whom a system affects over time, either directly or indirectly. For example, in a stock control system, the warehouse manager, the salesperson, and the customer are all involved in the process. It is crucial to determine who the users are by identifying these diverse groups.

**4. What is Participatory Design?** Participatory design is a method where potential users are involved completely in the design process from the very beginning. This allows designers to gain a thorough understanding of users’ demands and work contexts. Beyond design excellence, it helps users "own" the design and turn them into advocates for the final system.

**5. Define a "Persona" in the context of user focus.** A persona is a detailed depiction of a fictional individual who embodies the primary user base of a system. These characters are developed using data from research, including demographics, goals, habits, and challenges. The design team uses personas to ask, "How would this person react to this?" when proposing solutions.

**6. What is a "Scenario" in interaction design?** Scenarios are rich stories or tales that explain how customers interact with a product or service. They provide a thorough understanding of the user journey by outlining the context, objectives, and specific actions users take in a given situation. They are among the most versatile and potent design representations.

**7. Define "Navigation Design."** Navigation design focuses on how a user moves through an application or device. It involves understanding local structure—looking from one screen or page out—and global structure, which covers the overall site architecture and movement between screens. It ensures the user knows where they are in the interaction.

**8. What is the difference between "Hierarchical Organization" and "Dialog" in global structure?** Hierarchy connects logical groupings of screens or pages, often based on functional boundaries or user types. Dialog, on the other hand, refers to the pattern of interactions and the flow of screens and commands that occur during specific tasks, such as editing a record. Dialog captures loops and various paths through a system.

**9. Define "Affordance" in screen design.** Affordance is the concept that the physical or visual characteristics of an object indicate what can be done with it. For example, a handle is for pulling and a button is for pushing. Designers leverage these to develop intuitive features, though they are often culturally and historically specific rather than inherent.

**10. What is "Universal Design"?** Universal design is the practice of designing products so they can be used by as many people as possible in as many situations as possible. It aims to ensure that interactive systems are accessible on any technology platform and to users of all skill levels. This avoids excluding anyone through specific design decisions.

**11. Define "Iterative Design."** Iterative design is a deliberate process that cycles through multiple concepts, gradually improving the final result with each iteration. It acknowledges that requirements cannot be fully specified at the outset and must be refined through building and evaluating prototypes. Each cycle incorporates feedback to address insufficient specifications.

**12. What is "Usability Engineering"?** Usability engineering focuses on precisely defining the standards and metrics by which a product will be evaluated for its usability. It involves including a usability specification as part of the requirements phase, using system attributes as barometers for measurement. Standard metrics include time to complete a task and error rates.

**13. Define "Verification" in the software life cycle.** Verification is the process of reviewing a design to ensure it is complete and internally consistent. It is often described as building "the thing right". It involves checking that the refined design description maintains the behavior suggested by high-level descriptions and that no violations of system invariants occur.

**14. Define "Validation" in the software life cycle.** Validation is the process of demonstrating that the design meets the actual requirements and needs of the customer. It is described as building "the right thing". Unlike verification, validation is often subjective because it must bridge the "formality gap" between unstructured real-world needs and formal design.

**15. What is the "Formality Gap"?** The formality gap is the inherent difference and jump between unstructured real-world requirements and any formal, structured development approach. Because customer expectations originate in an ambiguous real world, objective mathematical proof of meeting those needs is impossible. Validation depends on subjective methods to bridge this gap.

**16. What is the "Waterfall Model" of software development?** The waterfall model is a classical software life cycle where each activity flows into the next in a sequential cascade. It typically includes requirements specification, architectural design, detailed design, coding, integration, and maintenance. While simple, it often fails to account for the iterative nature of modern interactive design.

**17. Define "Agile" development.** Agile is an incremental, iterative software development methodology that prioritizes flexibility, customer collaboration, and team interactions over rigid processes and documentation. Projects progress through short iterations or "sprints," delivering functional product increments frequently. It values responding to change over following a strict predefined plan.

**18. What is "DevOps"?** DevOps is a culture and set of practices designed to enhance coordination between software development (Dev) and IT operations (Ops). It focuses on automating and optimising the processes of building, testing, and deploying software to produce high-quality applications quickly. Key principles include continuous integration and continuous delivery.

**19. Define "HCI Patterns."** HCI patterns are invariant solutions to recurring design problems in a particular setting. They capture the essential characteristics of excellent design and advise the designer on what must be done and why. Patterns are readable, intuitive, and can be joined together to create a full design language.

**20. What is "Task Decomposition"?** Task decomposition is the process of examining how a single large task is divided into smaller subtasks and the sequence in which they are performed. A common technique is Hierarchical Task Analysis (HTA), which results in a hierarchy of subtasks and plans for their execution.

**21. Define "Knowledge-Based Task Analysis."** Knowledge-based task analysis involves building taxonomies of all the items and behaviors involved in a task. It aims to understand the specific knowledge a user requires to perform a task effectively. This helps in creating training materials and evaluating how knowledge is shared across different tasks.

**22. What are "Design Rules" in HCI?** Design rules are guidelines that a designer can follow to improve the final usability of a software product. They vary in authority and generality, ranging from abstract principles with low authority to highly specific and authoritative standards. They help designers assess the impact of their choices on usability.

**23. Define "Heuristic Evaluation."** Heuristic evaluation is a usability engineering method where several experts independently analyze a system against a set of simple rules or "heuristics". The goal is to identify deviations from these heuristics that might indicate potential usability problems. It is often considered a "cheap" and flexible usability method.

**24. What is a "Prototype"?** A prototype is a technical artefact that mimics or animates some but not all of the features of a desired system. It is used for ideation, testing, and evaluation during the design process. Prototypes can range from low-fidelity paper sketches to high-fidelity interactive mockups and simulations.

**25. Define "Software Quality Assurance" (SQA).** Software Quality Assurance is a systematic process used to ensure that software products and development processes adhere to specified quality standards. It involves various techniques, including static testing (code reviews) and dynamic testing (unit and system testing), to deliver reliable and user-friendly products.

### **Part 2: 10 Long-Answer Questions (Theories, Models, & Processes)**

**1. Describe the key stages of the Interaction Design Process.** The interaction design process is iterative and centered on the user. According to the text, the stages include:

- **Requirements:** Identifying what is wanted and what current behaviors exist through observation and interviewing.
- **Analysis:** Ordering findings from the requirements stage to highlight key points for the design team.
- **Design:** Moving from wanting something to determining how it will be done, guided by design rules and principles.
- **Iteration and Prototyping:** Evaluating designs with actual people to identify areas for improvement and refining them through multiple cycles.
- **Implementation and Deployment:** Creating the actual functioning system, including code, hardware, and documentation.

**2. Explain the levels of interaction within an application structure.** When designing the navigation and structure of a system, a user interacts at several distinct levels:

- **Widgets:** The selection of appropriate individual elements like buttons, menus, and text fields.
- **Screens or Windows:** The logical grouping of buttons and information on a single display area.
- **Navigation within the Application:** The flow and local structure that allows a user to understand where they are in the interaction.
- **Environment:** How the application interacts with external factors like the operating system, file storage, and other programs.

**3. Detail the seven principles of Universal Design.** Universal design provides a framework for creating systems accessible to the widest range of users:

- **Equitable Use:** The design is useful and marketable to people with diverse abilities.
- **Flexibility in Use:** The design accommodates a wide range of individual preferences and abilities.
- **Simple and Intuitive Use:** Use of the design is easy to understand, regardless of user experience or language skills.
- **Perceptible Information:** The design communicates necessary information effectively to the user, regardless of ambient conditions.
- **Tolerance for Error:** The design minimizes hazards and the adverse consequences of accidental actions.
- **Low Physical Effort:** The design can be used efficiently and comfortably with minimum fatigue.
- **Size and Space for Approach and Use:** Appropriate size and space is provided for approach, reach, and manipulation.

**4. Discuss the limitations of Prototyping using the "Hill-Climbing" analogy.** Prototyping is often a "hill-climbing" strategy, which has specific drawbacks:

- **Local Maxima:** By making small improvements, a designer may reach the top of a small "hill" (a local maximum) but miss a much larger "mountain" (a better design) because it required starting from a different point.
- **Necessity of a Good Starting Point:** Because you only move "uphill" from where you start, a poor initial concept may lead to a design that is only a refined version of a bad idea.
- **Understanding Faults:** Prototyping only works if the designer truly understands what is wrong and how to improve it.

**5. Explain Shneiderman’s Eight Golden Rules of Interface Design.** These rules provide a checklist for effective interface design:

- **Consistency:** Strive for uniform terminology, commands, and layout.
- **Enable Shortcuts:** Provide accelerators for frequent users to complete tasks quickly.
- **Informative Feedback:** Offer system responses proportional to the user's action.
- **Design Dialogs to Yield Closure:** Group actions into sequences with a clear beginning and end.
- **Offer Error Prevention:** Design the system to prevent errors and provide simple handling if they occur.
- **Permit Easy Reversal of Actions:** Allow "undo" to relieve user anxiety.
- **Support Internal Locus of Control:** Make users feel they are in charge of the system.
- **Reduce Short-Term Memory Load:** Keep displays simple and provide time for learning.

**6. Describe Norman’s Seven Principles for simplifying difficult tasks.** Donald Norman's principles focus on the relationship between user psychology and design:

- **Use Knowledge in the Head and World:** Combine internal memory with external cues.
- **Simplify Task Structure:** Reduce the need for complex problem solving.
- **Make Things Visible:** Ensure users see how their actions affect the system.
- **Get Mappings Right:** Align controls clearly with user goals.
- **Exploit Constraints:** Use physical or logical limitations to prevent incorrect actions.
- **Design for Error:** Assume errors will happen and plan for recovery.
- **Standardize When Necessary:** Use consistent mappings if natural ones aren't possible.

**7. Outline the phases of the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) Waterfall Model.** The waterfall model is characterized by a sequential flow of activities:

- **Requirements Specification:** Defining the desired functionality and work domain from the customer's perspective.
- **Architectural Design:** High-level decomposition of the system into component parts and resource sharing descriptions.
- **Detailed Design:** Refinement of architectural components into a description detailed enough for programming.
- **Coding and Unit Testing:** Implementing the design in a programming language and verifying individual units.
- **Integration and Testing:** Combining components and verifying the behavior of the integrated system.
- **Maintenance:** Correcting errors and modifying services after release.

**8. Explain the "formality gap" in validation and verification.** Bridging the gap between the real world and mathematical logic is a major design challenge:

- **Verification (The System Perspective):** Occurs within or between formal languages; it focuses on internal consistency and completeness.
- **Validation (The User Perspective):** Involves moving from a structured design back to unstructured real-world requirements.
- **Subjective Evidence:** Because real-world ambiguity cannot be fully captured in formal logic, validation must rely on subjective proofs and expert evaluation.
- **Notation Support:** Design notations must close this gap by being easy for experts to interpret psychologically.

**9. Discuss the principles and benefits of Agile development.** Agile focuses on speed and responsiveness to user needs:

- **Core Principles:** Valuing individuals over processes, working software over documentation, and customer collaboration over contracts.
- **Iterative Sprints:** Development happens in short cycles that deliver shippable increments.
- **Customer Satisfaction:** Continuous feedback ensures the product meets user expectations.
- **Quality and Morale:** Emphasis on testing and cross-functional collaboration improves code quality and team engagement.

**10. Describe the components and utility of a Usability Specification.** As part of usability engineering, a specification precisely defines measurable goals:

- **Measuring Concept:** A tangible interpretation of an abstract attribute (e.g., "undo an error").
- **Measuring Method:** The specific metric used, such as counting user actions.
- **Levels of Performance:** The specification sets a "Now Level" (current performance), a "Worst Case" (minimum acceptable), a "Planned Level" (target), and a "Best Case" (ideal).
- **Utility:** It provides a clear distinction between acceptable and unacceptable results for the final product.

### **Part 3: 6 Applied/Design Tasks (Scenarios & Analysis)**

**1. Developing a User Persona**

- **Task:** Create a persona for a new inventory management system.
- **Steps:**
    1. **Research:** Interview current warehouse staff to understand demographics and goals.
    2. **Identify Traits:** Note specific challenges, such as physical restrictions or technophobia.
    3. **Synthesize:** Create a fictional character (e.g., "Betty") with a specific age, job history, and motivations.
    4. **Humanize:** Add personal anecdotes or quotes to make the character feel real to the design team.
    5. **Apply:** Use this character to test design choices by asking how they would respond to a specific interface element.

**2. Creating an Interaction Scenario**

- **Task:** Develop a scenario for a proposed mobile movie-sharing application.
- **Steps:**
    1. **Set the Scene:** Describe a user (e.g., "Brian") in a specific context, such as a lunchroom.
    2. **Define the Goal:** State what the user wants to achieve (e.g., invite a friend to see a film).
    3. **Narrate Actions:** Describe the step-by-step physical and system interactions, such as scrolling and pressing buttons.
    4. **Include Outcomes:** Show the result of the interaction, including social components like sharing audio via earphones.
    5. **Analyze:** Use the narrative to identify if the system supports the user's intentions at every step.

**3. Applying Screen Layout Principles (Grouping and Alignment)**

- **Task:** Design a digital order form for a hardware store.
- **Steps:**
    1. **Logical Grouping:** Place related items, such as billing and delivery details, together in distinct sections.
    2. **Visual Structure:** Use white space, boxes, or lines to separate different functional groups.
    3. **Sequence:** Arrange fields in the natural order a user would fill them out (e.g., Name -> Address -> Payment).
    4. **Alignment:** Left-align text labels for readability, and align numeric data at the decimal point or right margin.
    5. **Scanning Support:** Ensure the "white space" or "counter" between elements allows the eye to scan rows without jumping.

**4. Performing Hierarchical Task Analysis (HTA)**

- **Task:** Analyze the task of "Making a Cup of Tea."
- **Textual Visual Flow:**
    - **Root Task 0:** Make cups of tea.
    - **Plan 0:** Do 1; if pot full do 2; then 3-4; then 5.
    - **Subtask 1:** Boil water.
    - **Sub-subtasks for 1:** 1.1 Fill kettle -> 1.2 Put kettle on hob -> 1.3 Light gas -> 1.4 Wait to boil -> 1.5 Turn off gas.
    - **Subtask 3:** Make pot.
    - **Sub-subtasks for 3:** 3.1 Warm pot -> 3.2 Put tea in pot -> 3.3 Pour in water.
    - **Subtask 5:** Pour tea.

**5. Establishing a Usability Specification**

- **Task:** Define a usability goal for a new "Undo" feature on a remote control.
- **Steps:**
    1. **Identify Attribute:** Choose "Backward Recoverability".
    2. **Define Measurement Concept:** Define it as the ability to cancel an erroneous programming sequence.
    3. **Define Measurement Method:** Count the number of button presses required to return to the initial state.
    4. **Set Levels:**
        - **Now Level:** No current ability to undo.
        - **Worst Case:** Actions equal to the number of mistakes made.
        - **Planned Level:** Maximum of two actions.
        - **Best Case:** One single "Cancel" action.

**6. Navigation Design using Hierarchy and Network**

- **Task:** Design the global structure for a messaging application.
- **Steps:**
    1. **Functional Hierarchy:** Create a top-down tree showing major areas: Info, Management, and Messages.
    2. **Dialog Network:** Use a network diagram to show how a user moves between specific screens, such as from the "Main Screen" to "Remove User".
    3. **Include Loops:** Ensure the diagram shows how the system returns to the main screen after a task is confirmed.
    4. **Check Constraints:** Ensure the hierarchy isn't too deep, aiming for breadth to facilitate visual search.
    5. **External Links:** Account for how the application interacts with the broader environment, such as the operating system's file management.
