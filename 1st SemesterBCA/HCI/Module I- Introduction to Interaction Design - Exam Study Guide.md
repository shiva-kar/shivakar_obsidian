
### **Part 1: 25 Short-Answer Questions (Definitions & Concepts)**

**1. Define Human-Computer Interaction (HCI).** **Answer:** HCI is a multidisciplinary field encompassing the design and utilization of computer technologies, with a primary focus on the intricate interplay between humans and computers. It entails the creation, testing, and assessment of interactive systems within the framework of user tasks and jobs. The field aims to ensure the effectiveness, efficiency, and user-friendliness of computation-based technologies for people and society.

**2. What is the definition of User Experience (UX) Design?** **Answer:** UX Design refers to all parts of a product as they are experienced by users, including landing pages, websites, the product itself, and related services. It is the method and technique used to create and execute products that enable beneficial and pertinent interactions. The ultimate goal is providing an experience that is relevant, efficient, and intuitive for the user.

**3. Define the concept of "Usability."** **Answer:** Usability is a crucial element of UX design focusing on a product's effectiveness and the degree of user satisfaction. It entails evaluating how well users can accomplish tasks and the intuitiveness of the design. Key guidelines for usability include learnability, efficiency, memorability, error prevention and recovery, and overall user satisfaction.

**4. What is a "Visual Angle" in human vision?** **Answer:** A visual angle is used to specify an image's size on the retina, determined by drawing lines from the top and bottom of an object to a central point on the front of the eye. It indicates how much of the object occupies the field of view. The measurement is typically expressed in degrees or minutes of arc.

**5. Define "Visual Acuity."** **Answer:** Visual acuity is the human capacity for fine detail perception. It is limited by the optical capabilities of the eye; for example, a person with normal eyesight can detect a line with a visual angle of 0.5 seconds of arc. Acuity increases with increased brightness but is restricted by the density of photoreceptors.

**6. What are "Rods" and "Cones"?** **Answer:** Rods and cones are photoreceptors found in the light-sensitive retina. Rods are highly sensitive to light, rule peripheral vision, and enable seeing in dimly lit environments, but they cannot resolve minute details. Cones, concentrated in the fovea, are less light-sensitive but enable color vision through three varieties sensitive to different light wavelengths.

**7. Explain the "Law of Size Constancy."** **Answer:** The law of size constancy states that our perception of an object's size remains unchanged regardless of changes in the object's visual angle on the retina. This occurs because the visual system uses depth cues, such as overlapping objects or familiarity, to interpret the relative distance and maintain a constant perception of size.

**8. What are "Saccades" and "Fixations" in the context of reading?** **Answer:** While reading, the eye performs quick, jerky movements known as saccades, which are followed by periods of relative stillness called fixations. Fixation periods make up approximately 94% of the time elapsed during reading, as this is the phase where actual visual perception and information intake take place.

**9. Define "Haptic Perception."** **Answer:** Haptic perception refers to the sense of touch, which provides information about the environment through sensory receptors in the skin. It includes feedback regarding temperature (thermoreceptors), pressure (mechanoreceptors), and pain (nociceptors). It is a crucial feedback channel, particularly when physical manipulation of objects is required.

**10. What is "Kinethesis"?** **Answer:** Kinethesis is a facet of haptic perception involving the awareness of the position and movement of one's own body and limbs. This sense is governed by receptors in the joints. It impacts both performance and comfort, providing essential feedback for tasks like touch typing by allowing users to sense finger positions.

**11. Define "Sensory Memory."** **Answer:** Sensory memory acts as a buffer for stimuli perceived through the senses, filtering information before it moves to short-term memory. Each sensory channel has a corresponding buffer: iconic memory for vision, echoic memory for hearing, and haptic memory for touch. Information in these buffers is transient and constantly overwritten by new stimuli.

**12. What is "Short-term Memory" (Working Memory)?** **Answer:** Short-term memory, or working memory, serves as a "scratchpad" for temporary information recall. It is used for tasks requiring immediate data, such as mental arithmetic or remembering the first part of a sentence while reading the rest. It has a limited capacity (7 ± 2 units) and degrades quickly, typically within 200 milliseconds.

**13. Define "Chunking."** **Answer:** Chunking is the process of organizing information into manageable clusters to boost the functional capacity of short-term memory. Since the human brain can recall approximately 7 ± 2 informational units, grouping related items (like numbers in a phone sequence) allows for the retention of more complex data within those limited memory slots.

**14. What is "Long-term Memory"?** **Answer:** Long-term memory is the primary resource for storing all factual data, experiential knowledge, and behavioral norms. Unlike short-term memory, it has an enormous, possibly infinite capacity and much slower access times (roughly 0.1 seconds). Information is stored there through rehearsal and does not deteriorate over time.

**15. Define "Semantic Memory" and "Episodic Memory."** **Answer:** Semantic memory is a structured repository of learned knowledge, including facts, concepts, and skills. Episodic memory is the sequential record of personal experiences and events, allowing individuals to reconstruct specific moments in their lives. Semantic knowledge is often derived from the information initially stored in episodic memory.

**16. What is a "Semantic Network"?** **Answer:** A semantic network is a model of long-term memory where knowledge is organized as a web of connected concepts. Classes link items, and child classes can inherit attributes from parent classes. This structure allows for inference and relationship representation, as knowledge is stored based on associations.

**17. Define "Deductive Reasoning."** **Answer:** Deductive reasoning is a logical process where specific conclusions are derived from stated premises. If the premises are true, the conclusion is logically necessary. For example, if it is Friday (premise 1) and she goes to work on Fridays (premise 2), the conclusion is she will go to work.

**18. Define "Inductive Reasoning."** **Answer:** Inductive reasoning involves extrapolating general knowledge about cases we haven't seen based on specific cases we have observed. It is inherently unreliable because a single negative instance can refute a general conclusion. Despite this, humans constantly use induction to learn about and categorize their environment.

**19. Define "Abductive Reasoning."** **Answer:** Abductive reasoning is the process of tying a specific fact to the behavior or condition that likely gave rise to it. It is used to generate explanations for observed events. For example, if we know a person drives fast when they have been drinking, we might abductively assume they have been drinking if we see them driving fast.

**20. What is "Problem Space Theory"?** **Answer:** Proposed by Newell and Simon, this theory views problem-solving as a search through a "problem space" consisting of various states. Users use "operators" to transition from an initial state to a goal state. Because spaces can be vast, humans use heuristics like "means-ends analysis" to select the best operators.

**21. Define "Personas."** **Answer:** Personas are fictional characters created to represent different user types within a targeted demographic or behavioral pattern. They represent the requirements, goals, and traits of a specific user base. Components typically include demographics, goals, motivations, challenges, andhabits.

**22. Define "Scenarios."** **Answer:** Scenarios are stories or tales that explain how customers interact with a product or service within a specific context. They outline the user's goals, the actions taken to reach those goals, and the eventual outcome. Scenarios help designers understand the user journey and identify potential improvement areas.

**23. What is "Design Thinking"?** **Answer:** Design thinking is a human-centered innovation approach that integrates the needs of people, the possibilities of technology, and business requirements. It is an innovative process prioritizing design from a human perspective, solving problems through empathy, creativity, prototyping, and testing.

**24. Define "Ergonomics" (Human Factors).** **Answer:** Ergonomics is the study of the physical aspects of interaction, including the layout of the screen, the design of controls, and the physical environment. It focuses on user performance and how the interface contributes to or detracts from it. It addresses issues like control arrangement, lighting, and health safety.

**25. Define "WIMP."** **Answer:** WIMP stands for Windows, Icons, Menus, and Pointers. It is the primary interface style for most modern interactive computer systems, such as Microsoft Windows or MacOS. These elements—windows for independent areas, icons for representations, menus for options, and pointers for selection—collectively form a toolkit of interaction "widgets".

### **Part 2: 10 Long-Answer Questions (Theories, Models, & Processes)**

**1. Explain Norman’s Execution-Evaluation Cycle of Interaction.** **Answer:** Norman’s model is a highly influential interaction model that describes the communication between a user and a computer through seven stages, divided into two main phases:

- **Execution Phase:** This involves the user taking action to change the state of the system. It consists of:
    - **Establishing the goal:** Defining what is to be achieved.
    - **Forming the intention:** Deciding on the specific action to take.
    - **Specifying the action sequence:** Planning the physical steps.
    - **Executing the action:** Performing the physical interaction with the interface.
- **Evaluation Phase:** This involves the user assessing the results of their action. It consists of:
    - **Perceiving the system state:** Seeing what happened on the screen/interface.
    - **Interpreting the system state:** Making sense of the changes based on expectations.
    - **Evaluating the system state:** Comparing the result against the original goals.
- **Gulfs of Interaction:** Norman uses this model to identify the **Gulf of Execution** (the difference between the user's formulation of actions and the actions permitted by the system) and the **Gulf of Evaluation** (the gap between the physical presentation of the system and the user's expectations).

**2. Describe the General Interaction Framework and its four main components.** **Answer:** The general interaction framework expands on Norman's cycle by explicitly including the system as a distinct component. It identifies four nodes, each with its own language:

- **The System (S):** Uses a **core language** to describe computational characteristics.
- **The User (U):** Uses a **task language** to describe psychological attributes of the domain.
- **The Input (I):** Part of the interface where the user provides instructions.
- **The Output (O):** Part of the interface where the system presents results.
- **The Four Translations:** Interaction is viewed as a series of translations between these components:
    - **Articulation:** The user translates their goal into the input language.
    - **Performance:** The input is translated into the system's core language.
    - **Presentation:** The system's state change is translated into an output display.
    - **Observation:** The user interprets the output to evaluate the result.

**3. Outline the Evolution of HCI from early computing to the present.** **Answer:** HCI has undergone several major phases characterized by technological shifts and design paradigms:

- **Early Computing (1940s-50s):** Limited interaction via **punch cards** and command-line interfaces for military and scientific use.
- **Mainframe/Terminal Computing (1960s-70s):** Introduction of **text-based terminals** and standardized command languages.
- **Personal Computing & GUIs (1980s):** Emergence of the **Graphical User Interface (GUI)**, popularised by the Apple Macintosh and the Xerox Star.
- **World Wide Web (1990s):** Introduction of the **hyperlink model** and web browsers, changing information access.
- **Ubiquitous/Mobile Computing (2000s-Present):** Shift toward **smartphones**, **wearables**, and **touch-based** interaction, embedding computing into everyday life.
- **Current/Future Trends:** Focus on **Gesture-Based Interfaces**, **Voice/Conversational AI**, and **Explainable/Ethical AI**.

**4. Detail the structure and function of the Human Memory system in HCI.** **Answer:** The human memory system is categorized into three functional roles, each impacting interface design:

- **Sensory Memory:** Acts as a buffer for sensory stimuli. **Iconic memory** (visual) lasts about 0.5 seconds, while **echoic memory** (auditory) allows for brief "playback" of sounds to determine direction or meaning.
- **Short-term (Working) Memory:** A limited-capacity "scratchpad" (7 ± 2 units) used for immediate tasks. Designers must minimize the **memory load** on users, as information degrades within 200ms and is easily disrupted by interference.
- **Long-term Memory:** A permanent repository for knowledge, including **semantic memory** (facts/concepts) and **episodic memory** (events). Knowledge is stored via **rehearsal** and is optimized when information is structured, familiar, and concrete.

**5. Compare Gestalt Theory and Problem Space Theory in human problem-solving.** **Answer:**

- **Gestalt Theory:** Emerging in the early 20th century, it argues that problem-solving involves both **insight** and **productive restructuring** of the problem. It highlights the risk of **fixation**, where reproductive problem-solving (relying on prior experience) prevents users from seeing novel solutions.
- **Problem Space Theory:** Developed by Newell and Simon, it views the mind as a limited information processor searching through a **problem space**. It relies on **operators** to move between states and uses **heuristics** (like means-ends analysis) to navigate large spaces efficiently.
- **Key Difference:** While Gestalt emphasizes sudden "aha!" moments of insight, Problem Space Theory emphasizes a structured, step-by-step search process constrained by the limits of short-term memory.

__6. Explain the process of Skill Acquisition according to Anderson’s ACT_ model._* **Answer:** Skill acquisition involves transitioning through three distinct levels of proficiency:

- **Level 1 (Declarative):** The learner uses **general-purpose rules** to interpret facts. This stage is slow and requires significant **memory access**.
- **Level 2 (Knowledge Compilation):** The learner creates task-specific rules through **proceduralisation**. This process eliminates the need for constant memory retrieval by substituting variables with fixed values.
- **Level 3 (Tuning):** Rules are refined through **generalisation**. The user develops general-purpose rules that condense commonalities across tasks, leading to **expert behavior** that is routine and efficient.

**7. Discuss the various Input/Output Channels and their significance in HCI.** **Answer:** Humans interact with the world through sensory inputs and motor effectors:

- **Sight (Vision):** The primary source of information. Designers must consider **visual acuity**, color perception (hue, intensity, saturation), and limitations like **color blindness**.
- **Hearing (Auditory):** Provides critical feedback through pitch, loudness, and timbre. It is useful for **alerts** and **system status** information, such as the "cocktail party effect" which allows users to filter background noise.
- **Touch (Haptic):** Provides feedback through pressure and temperature. It is essential for **kinethesis** (knowing limb position) and is vital for users with visual impairments through **Braille interfaces**.
- **Effectors:** Limbs, fingers, and the voice system used to provide input to the computer via typing, mouse control, or speech.

**8. Describe the "Five Dimensions of Interaction Design" and their impact on UX.** _(Note: Based on source text under "Interaction Design Basics")_ **Answer:** Interaction Design (IxD) concentrates on shaping user experiences through several key dimensions:

- **Visceral:** The immediate, subconscious reaction to the aesthetic of the product.
- **Behavioural:** The practical experience of using the product and how it functions.
- **Reflective:** The long-term conscious evaluation of the product's value and meaning.
- **Cultural:** How the design fits into the user's social and cultural context.
- **Functional:** The actual tasks the product allows the user to complete.
- **Impact:** By addressing these dimensions alongside **affordances** (clues to use) and **signifiers** (indicators of where to act), designers create interfaces that are intuitive and satisfying.

**9. Summarize the major History Paradigms of Interaction.** **Answer:** Key advancements in interaction have redefined the human-computer relationship:

- **Time-sharing (1960s):** Allowed a single computer to serve many users, enabling **interactive programming** rather than batch sessions.
- **Video Display Units (1950s-60s):** Shifted the medium from paper to screens; Sutherland's **Sketchpad** showed that computers could manipulate visual abstractions.
- **Programming Toolkits (1960s):** Engelbart's "Augmenting Man's Intellect" introduced the **mouse** and **word processing**, viewing computing as a "toolbox".
- **Personal Computing (1970s):** Alan Kay’s vision of the **Dynabook** and the development of **Smalltalk** brought powerful tools to the general public.
- **WIMP & Hypertext (1980s-90s):** Allowed for multitasking and **non-linear browsing**, leading to the World Wide Web.

**10. Explain the "Principles of Design Thinking."** **Answer:** Design Thinking is a five-step iterative process focused on the human perspective:

- **Step 1: Empathize:** Understand the emotions and needs of the users by observing them in their natural settings.
- **Step 2: Define:** Create a meaningful and actionable **problem statement** that identifies what is wrong and how to fix it.
- **Step 3: Ideate:** Question assumptions and generate a wide range of potential solutions without judgment.
- **Step 4: Prototype:** Create low-fidelity (sketches/wireframes) or high-fidelity models to test functionality and user flow.
- **Step 5: Test:** Observe real users interacting with the prototype to identify usability issues and refine the design.

### **Part 3: 6 Applied/Design Tasks (Scenarios & Analysis)**

**Task 1: Design a Persona for a new Mobile Banking App.** **Process:**

1. **Research:** Conduct interviews and surveys with real banking customers to gather data on demographics and habits.
2. **Segmentation:** Group users into categories (e.g., "Tech-Savvy Student," "Retired Non-Technical User").
3. **Persona Development:** Detail a fictional character like "David, 65," who prefers physical branches but wants to see his balance at home.
4. **Include Goals:** Note David’s motivations (security) and pain points (small font sizes).
5. **Validation:** Review the persona with stakeholders to ensure it accurately represents a segment of the user base.

**Task 2: Develop a Scenario for a "Smart Refrigerator" shopping task.** **Process:**

1. **Set the Context:** Describe the environment (e.g., "The kitchen on a busy Monday morning").
2. **Define User Goal:** The user needs to know if they are out of milk without opening the fridge.
3. **Outline Actions:** Describe the specific interaction: the user taps the fridge's outer screen, views the inventory list, and clicks "add to grocery list".
4. **Describe the Outcome:** The user feels satisfied because the task was completed in seconds.
5. **Iterate:** Use this scenario to determine if the "add to list" button is visible enough for the user.

**Task 3: Apply the "Human Information Processing" model to analyze a distracted driver.** **Diagram/Description:**

1. **Input:** Sensory stimuli (A red light and a ringing phone).
2. **Perceptual Processor:** The driver sees the red light (VIS) and hears the phone (ACU).
3. **Cognitive Processor:** The driver's **Working Memory** is overloaded. The cognitive system must choose between the goal of "stop at light" and "answer phone".
4. **Memory Access:** The driver retrieves the "red means stop" rule from **Long-term Memory**.
5. **Motor Processor:** The cognitive system sends a signal to the motor system to "press the brake".
6. **Output:** The driver's foot moves to the brake pedal.

**Task 4: Identify and correct a "Gulf of Evaluation" in a microwave interface.** **Process:**

1. **Identify the Problem:** A user sets the microwave to 1 minute, but the display only shows a rotating "busy" icon instead of a countdown.
2. **Analyze the Gap:** The user cannot perceive the system state (time remaining) and cannot evaluate if the action was successful.
3. **Design Intervention:** Add a digital countdown timer to the display.
4. **Result:** The "Presentation" translation now matches the user's "Observation" requirements, closing the Gulf of Evaluation.

**Task 5: Organize a Control Panel using Ergonomic principles.** **Process:**

1. **Group Related Functions:** Place all "Temperature" controls in one section and "Timer" controls in another.
2. **Sequential Arrangement:** Place the "Power On" button at the top-left (start) and the "Start Cycle" button at the bottom-right (finish).
3. **Frequency of Use:** Place the "Volume" dial in the most accessible central area if it is adjusted most often.
4. **Physical Position:** Ensure all displays are at the user's eye level to prevent neck strain.

**Task 6: Use "Chunking" to improve the design of a Registration Form.** **Process:**

1. **Identify the Load:** A long form with 20 fields (Name, Address, Credit Card, etc.) overloads short-term memory.
2. **Apply Chunking:** Group the 20 fields into three distinct "chunks": "Personal Info," "Shipping Address," and "Payment Details".
3. **Provide Closure:** Use a "Next" button after each chunk so the user feels a sense of task completion ("Closure").
4. **Outcome:** The user's cognitive burden is reduced to 3 units (the chunks) rather than 20 individual items.
