### **Part 1: 25 Short-Answer Questions (Definitions & Concepts)**

**1. What is a Speech Interface?** A speech interface is the software that enables communication between voice-activated devices and human beings. These interfaces have long attracted researchers who aim to allow computers to talk for themselves and interact naturally with users. Successful programs in this domain are those that meet user expectations for quick tasks while requiring minimal cognitive load and producing few errors.

**2. Define Ubiquitous Computing.** Ubiquitous computing is a paradigm where information processing is integrated into every everyday task or object. Unlike traditional desktop computing, it allows for computing in any format, at any time, and from any location. It involves embedding microprocessors into electronic devices to facilitate constant information communication and increase user productivity.

**3. What is the significance of the HAL 9000 in HCI history?** The HAL 9000 is a fictional computer from the film _2001: A Space Odyssey_ that set a benchmark for computer performance expectations among science fiction fans and sophisticated developers. It represented the dream of a machine capable of informal conversation and lip-reading. However, modern achievements in speech processing are still considered startlingly complex compared to such fictional visions.

**4. Define Discrete-word Recognition.** Discrete-word recognition systems are designed to identify individual words spoken by a specific user. These systems typically achieve a reliability of 90% to 98% for vocabularies ranging from 100 to 1,000 words. They often require speaker-dependent training, where the user repeats the vocabulary to improve accuracy, particularly in quiet environments using head-mounted microphones.

**5. What is Speaker-Dependent Training?** Speaker-dependent training is a process where a speech recognition system is "trained" by having a specific user repeat the entire intended vocabulary once or twice. This method results in higher recognition accuracy compared to speaker-independent systems. It allows the software to adjust to the unique speech patterns, dialects, and intonations of an individual user.

**6. Define Continuous-speech Recognition.** Continuous-speech recognition refers to systems that can identify and transcribe speech spoken at a natural, fluid pace. These systems face significant challenges because typical speech patterns tend to obscure the boundaries between words. They must manage variable speaking rates, dialects, background noise, and the complex task of semantic interpretation for word disambiguation.

**7. What is Interactive Voice Response (IVR)?** Interactive Voice Response, or voice information systems, provides telephone-based information services for tasks like checking flight schedules or refilling prescriptions. Users are guided by voice prompts to press keys or speak simple commands to navigate menu trees. These systems are widely used because they offer 24-hour customer support at a relatively low cost for organisations.

**8. Define Information Visualisation.** Information visualisation is the use of 3D displays and virtual reality to represent scientific data and other complex information sets. By using interactive and animated representations, users can obtain a deeper understanding of data and discover patterns that are impossible to see in static formats. It helps in exploring abstract factors by mapping them onto spatial dimensions.

**9. What is a Virtual Wind Tunnel?** A virtual wind tunnel is a VR application where an engineer uses virtual reality goggles to view a simulated aircraft model. The system uses fluid dynamics equations to calculate and display air movements and pressure patterns. It allows engineers to study turbulence and specific details by moving a virtual baton that shoots virtual bubbles around the aircraft.

**10. Define Planar Graphs in visualisation.** Planar graphs are specific types of networks that can be arranged in a two-dimensional layout without any of the connecting lines crossing each other. They are often used in flow charts and network diagrams to maintain visual clarity. When networks become non-planar, designers often move to three-dimensional representations to reduce visual clutter and overlapping lines.

**11. What is a Camtree?** A Camtree is a 3D visualisation feature developed at Xerox PARC used to represent hierarchical information. It displays a hierarchy from left to right, with subordinate nodes grouped in a circle around the base of a cone. The cones can be rotated to bring specific nodes into focus, allowing for the visualisation of up to 10,000 nodes.

**12. Define Lifelines.** Lifelines is a visualisation technique used to display personal histories, such as medical or judicial records, using a model similar to a Gantt chart. It plots irregular events along a timeline axis, where different types of activity are represented by distinct lines. This allows an evaluator to see a comprehensive overview of a subject's history over time.

**13. What is Home Automation in the context of UbiCom?** Home automation is a ubiquitous computing application where all connectable devices in a house are linked to a local area network or the internet. Users instruct these gadgets through a "parent" device, such as an Apple Hub or Amazon Alexa. This requires providing processing power to every everyday object to make them part of a connected system.

**14. Define Radio Frequency Identification (RFID).** RFID stands for Radio Frequency Identification, a technology where tags interact passively with scanners to communicate data. These chips are used extensively in credit and debit cards to hold encrypted financial information. They are considered a core technology for ubiquitous computing as they allow objects to be identified and tracked automatically.

**15. What is the SMART DEI Framework?** The SMART DEI framework is a holistic model for studying and creating ubiquitous computing systems. It stands for Smart Devices, Environments, and Interactions. It focuses on three design approaches: multifunctional mobile portals (Smart Devices), physical infrastructure with sensors (Smart Environments), and the use of intelligent agents for engagement (Smart Interaction).

**16. Define Smart Devices within the DEI model.** Smart devices are multifunctional ICT devices, such as mobile phones, that serve as portals to various application services. They are typically personalised to a specific owner and house the user interface and locus of control. Their primary features include mobility, dynamic service discovery, and the ability to access resources intermittently across networks.

**17. Define Tabs, Pads, and Boards.** Tabs, Pads, and Boards are Weiser’s original three device sizes for ubiquitous computing. Tabs are centimeter-sized wearable devices for tracking; Pads are decimeter-sized hand-held devices for accessing mobile services; and Boards are meter-sized displays used for group viewing or as horizontal surface computers. They represent the shift toward portable and less noticeable computing.

**18. What is Smart Dust (MEMS)?** Smart Dust refers to Micro ElectroMechanical Systems (MEMS) that range in size from nanometers to millimetres. These microscopic devices can integrate electronic and mechanical components but often lack visual displays. They enable ubiquitous computing by being integrated into laptops to detect falls or by being painted onto surfaces to create networked sensors.

**19. Define Proxy-based Service Access.** Proxy-based service access uses a client proxy to offload network and presentation processing from low-resource mobile devices to a more powerful node. The proxy can simplify message protocols, aggregate content from multiple providers, and handle communication with web servers. This allows devices to function even when they are intermittently disconnected from the network.

**20. What is Ethical HCI?** Ethical HCI is the practice of prioritising user-centered values and responsible conduct during the development and application of interactive technologies. It involves addressing issues like privacy, informed consent, accessibility, and algorithmic bias. It also considers the long-term impact of technology on user well-being and environmental sustainability.

**21. Define Informed Consent in HCI.** Informed consent is an ethical requirement where users must be made aware of how interactive systems acquire their data. Users should have the power to decide whether their data is collected and how it will be used. It ensures that users maintain autonomy and are not subjected to surveillance without their express authorisation.

**22. What is Algorithmic Bias?** Algorithmic bias occurs when interactive systems rely on algorithms that sustain prejudices present in their training data. This can lead to unfair or discriminatory results for certain user groups. Ethical design requires identifying and eliminating these biases to ensure that digital outcomes are equitable for all users regardless of background.

**23. Define Dark Patterns.** Dark patterns are design cues used in user interfaces that are intentionally crafted to lead users into making decisions that are not in their best interests. Avoiding these patterns is a key component of ethical HCI, as they violate the principle of respecting user autonomy. Ethical design prioritises honest communication over coercive engagement.

**24. What are End-of-Life Considerations in HCI?** End-of-life considerations involve the ethical and environmental impact of disposing of electronic gadgets. This includes managing e-waste through responsible recycling and the use of sustainable materials during production. Ethical HCI practitioners must design systems with environmentally conscious practices to minimise the ecological footprint of digital technology.

**25. Define Otsu Binarisation.** Otsu binarisation is a methodology used in hand gesture recognition that automatically determines a threshold value for a bimodal image from its histogram. All pixels in a grayscale image are converted to either black or white based on this threshold. This process produces a binary image with improved accuracy, which is essential for detecting object contours and convexity flaws.

### **Part 2: 10 Long-Answer Questions (Theories, Models, & Processes)**

**1. Discuss the core challenges and obstacles associated with Speech Output.** The textbook identifies several significant hurdles that designers must overcome when implementing voice output in interactive systems:

- **Slow Pace:** Speech output is inherently slower than visual displays, as it takes more time to listen to a sentence than to scan it visually.
- **Ephemeral Nature:** Unlike text on a screen, which remains visible for review, speech is temporary and disappears as soon as it is uttered.
- **Scanning and Searching:** It is extremely difficult for a user to "scan" or search through spoken segments to find specific information compared to the ease of navigating a visual document.
- **Cognitive Load:** Listening to speech requires "acoustic memory," which uses the same mental resources required for problem-solving and planning.
- **Environmental Interference:** Background noise in office or public settings can make it difficult for users to hear output clearly or maintain confidentiality.

**2. Outline the specific opportunities and scenarios where Speech Technology is most beneficial.** Despite its drawbacks, speech interfaces are highly effective in specialized domains where traditional input/output is impractical:

- **Visual Impairments:** It provides a primary mode of interaction for users who cannot access visual displays.
- **Hands-Busy Situations:** It is ideal for operators, such as aviation engine inspectors, who must use their hands for physical tasks while interacting with a computer.
- **Mobility Requirements:** Speech allows users to remain mobile and walk around their environment rather than being tethered to a keyboard.
- **Eyes-Occupied Tasks:** In scenarios like surgery or piloting, where the user's eyes must remain fixed on a task, speech provides a secondary channel for data entry.
- **Harsh/Cramped Conditions:** It is useful in underwater or rescue operations where the physical environment precludes the use of a standard keyboard.

**3. Explain the difficulties faced by Continuous-speech Recognition systems.** Achieving high-accuracy dictation is a massive research challenge due to the following factors:

- **Word Boundaries:** Normal human speech patterns often blur the boundaries between words, making it hard for software to distinguish them.
- **Variability:** Recognition must account for different speaking rates, regional dialects, and the shifting emotional intonation of the speaker.
- **Contextual Ambiguity:** Systems often struggle to disambiguate words that sound identical (e.g., "Houston" vs. "Austin" or "wreck a nice beach" vs. "recognise speech").
- **Cognitive Disruption:** The mental effort required for dictation can disrupt the user's ability to plan complex sentence structures, often lowering the quality of the written work.
- **Error Remediation:** Fixing errors in transcribed text using only voice is slow and challenging compared to keyboard editing.

**4. Describe the three categories of 3D Scientific and Technical Data Visualisation.** Information visualisation can be classified by how it maps data dimensions onto virtual space:

- **Physical Realism:** All three virtual dimensions match actual spatial dimensions, such as a virtual wind tunnel where airflows around an aircraft are simulated.
- **Mixed Dimensions:** Two dimensions represent physical space (like a map), while the third represents abstract data (like rainfall levels) at each specific point.
- **Abstract Mapping:** One or none of the spatial dimensions match reality, such as a 3D spreadsheet graph where the X, Y, and Z axes represent independent numerical variables.
- **Structured Mapping:** Using discrete structures like hierarchies (Camtrees) or networks to organise non-numerical data like file systems or hypertext links.

**5. Detail Weiser’s extended forms for ubiquitous ICT devices: Dust, Skin, and Clay.** Beyond the macro-sized tabs and pads, modern UbiCom research explores even more integrated forms:

- **Smart Dust:** These are MEMS (Micro ElectroMechanical Systems) that range from nanometers to millimetres in size. They can be integrated into lap-tops to detect falls or used as tiny sensors in the environment.
- **Smart Skin:** This involves painting MEMS devices onto various surfaces or using conductive polymers to create display surfaces on flexible materials like clothing or curtains.
- **Smart Clay:** This refers to ensembles of MEMS that can be shaped into any desired three-dimensional form to create artefacts that mimic physical real-world objects.

**6. Categorize the five types of Mobility for ubiquitous computing devices.** Mobile gadgets are defined by how they are carried or integrated with the human user:

- **Accompanied:** Portable devices, like smartphones, that are carried in clothing or bags but are not strictly "worn".
- **Portable:** Larger devices, like laptops, designed to be operated with two hands while the user is seated.
- **Hand-held:** Small devices, like gaming consoles or mobile offices, that are typically used with one hand or hands-free.
- **Wearable:** Accessories like smartwatches, glasses, or contact lenses that are managed hands-free.
- **Implanted:** Silicon or carbon-based devices embedded in the body, such as pacemakers or nanotechnology, to improve physical or cognitive functions.

**7. Describe the phases of the Service Provision Life Cycle in ubiquitous systems.** Distributed services for smart devices must be managed through four continuous stages:

- **Creation:** Service processes register with directories, and requesters look for and configure the necessary components.
- **Execution:** Services are triggered, and multiple interconnected services are coordinated to perform the intended task.
- **Maintenance:** Descriptions are updated, and the composition of services is reconfigured to adapt to changing environments.
- **Dissolution:** Services are briefly suspended or permanently withdrawn and removed from registration.
- **Textual Diagram Flow:** Creation -> Execution <-> Maintenance -> Dissolution.

**8. Discuss the ethical principles of Privacy and Informed Consent in HCI.** These principles form the bedrock of responsible interaction design:

- **Data Collection Limits:** Systems should not track user behaviour or preferences without express permission, as this constitutes a violation of privacy.
- **Informed Awareness:** Users must be provided with clear information regarding what data is being collected and the intentions behind its use.
- **Locus of Control:** Users should have the power to decide and manage their own engagement with the system and its data practices.
- **Transparency:** Designers should provide open communication about system operations to build trust between the user and the technology.
- **Anti-Coercion:** Designs should respect user autonomy and avoid using "dark patterns" to manipulate consent.

**9. Outline the methodology for Hand Gesture Recognition in HCI.** A reliable system for tracking static and dynamic hand movements involves several technical steps:

- **Image Capture:** A camera module captures a real-time image of the user's hand gesture.
- **Grayscale Conversion:** The image is converted into grayscale and treated to reduce noise for a smoother result.
- **Binarisation:** Otsu binarisation determines a threshold to convert the image into a high-accuracy binary (black and white) format.
- **Contour Extraction:** The system detects object shapes and identifies convexity flaws and the convex hull.
- **Classification:** A Haar cascade classifier, trained on various lighting angles, identifies the specific gesture (e.g., a palm or a fist).
- **Action Mapping:** The detected gesture is transformed into a specific computer activity, such as launching an app.

**10. Explain the methodology for Gaze Gesture interaction using HMDs.** This advanced technology tracks eye movements at close range to determine user intent:

- **Near-Eye Imaging:** A camera built into a head-mounted display (HMD) takes images of the eye.
- **Filtering:** Pupil coordinates are generated, and the image is subjected to Gaussian filtering to prepare for analysis.
- **Neural Network Processing:** Two deep convolutional neural network modules categorise the gaze trajectories.
- **Synthetic-to-Real Mapping:** The system is trained to bridge the gap between synthetic data and real image distributions to ensure reliability.
- **Calibration:** The software adjusts to individual eye positions and manages different lighting conditions.
- **Command Execution:** Specific gaze patterns are assigned to navigation or user-specified functions.

### **Part 3: 6 Applied/Design Tasks (Scenarios & Analysis)**

**1. Evaluating a Speech Interface for a Drawing Program**

- **Scenario:** A designer wants to add voice commands to a mouse-driven CAD tool.
- **Steps to solve:**
    1. **Analyze Task Switching:** Identify if the user is frequently moving the cursor from the drawing area to a tool palette.
    2. **Select Vocabulary:** Define a small set of discrete words (e.g., "Line," "Circle," "Bold") to minimize cognitive load.
    3. **Implement Hybrid Input:** Maintain the mouse for spatial pointing while using speech for tool selection to increase performance by ~21%.
    4. **Mitigate Memory Load:** Ensure that the user does not have to plan complex sentences while drawing, as acoustic memory interferes with spatial reasoning.

**2. Designing a Home Automation Scenario**

- **Scenario:** Create a ubiquitous computing plan for a modern smart home.
- **Steps to solve:**
    1. **Identify Connected Objects:** Determine which devices (lights, fridge, security) will be networked via IP or RFID.
    2. **Define Control Hub:** Select a parent device (e.g., Google Home) to serve as the primary interaction portal.
    3. **Apply Context-Awareness:** Program the system to recognize authorised passengers or owners through smartphone proximity.
    4. **Automate Capture:** Use IP cameras to manage security and allow the home to monitor its own state.

**3. Visualising Structured Hierarchical Data (Camtree)**

- **Scenario:** A large corporation needs to visualise its 5,000-person organisation chart.
- **Steps to solve:**
    1. **Map Hierarchy:** Use a 3D Camtree model to avoid the space constraints of a 2D chart.
    2. **Arrange Nodes:** Place the parent node at the apex and group subordinates in a circle around the base of the cone.
    3. **Enable Interaction:** Allow users to rotate the cones to bring specific branches into the foreground for reading names.
    4. **Add Visual Cues:** Implement shadows and lighting to help the user perceive the depth and hidden areas of the tree.

**4. Conducting an Ethical Review for Data Collection**

- **Scenario:** An app developer is designing a new location-tracking feature.
- **Steps to solve:**
    1. **Seek Informed Consent:** Design a clear prompt explaining exactly what data is collected and why.
    2. **Verify Data Security:** Implement robust measures to protect user information from unauthorised breaches.
    3. **Check for Bias:** Ensure that the tracking algorithms do not inadvertently discriminate against users based on socioeconomic levels.
    4. **Provide Opt-Out:** Give users full control to disable tracking and manage their own digital footprint.

**5. Implementing a Hand Gesture Interface for a Smartwatch**

- **Scenario:** Create a hands-free navigation system for a small-screen wearable.
- **Steps to solve:**
    1. **Integrate Sensors:** Use infrared sensors or a camera module on the watch to capture gestures.
    2. **Image Processing:** Use grayscale conversion and Otsu binarisation to detect the hand's contour.
    3. **Gesture Classification:** Use a Haar cascade classifier to recognize a "palm" or "fist" movement.
    4. **Map Operations:** Assign simple gestures to common tasks like adjusting volume, skipping songs, or phoning a contact.

**6. Using Gaze Tracking for Commercial Research**

- **Scenario:** A marketing firm wants to see which part of an online advertisement catches the user's interest.
- **Steps to solve:**
    1. **Equip Headgear:** Have participants wear an HMD with a near-eye camera to track gaze direction in real-time.
    2. **Calibrate Tracking:** Use Gaussian filtering and pupil coordinates to map the user's focus precisely on the screen.
    3. **Analyze Trajectories:** Use deep convolutional neural networks to categorize where the user's eyes linger most.
    4. **Determine Product Interest:** Identify the specific visual elements or products that captured the user's attention based on gaze duration.
